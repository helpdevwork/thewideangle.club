Most people get a debit card before they understand what it actually does — it just arrives with a
new bank account. Before we get into comparing credit cards, it's worth being precise about what a
debit card is, because everything about credit cards makes more sense once this is clear.

## The one-line definition

A debit card is a plastic (or virtual) key to your own bank account. Every time you swipe, tap, or
enter your debit card details online, money moves directly out of your savings or current account —
instantly, and only if it's actually there.

## How it's different from cash, concretely

A debit card isn't really "borrowing" anything. If your account has ₹5,000, you can spend up to
₹5,000. Try to spend ₹5,001 and the transaction is simply declined (unless you have an overdraft
facility explicitly set up, which is a separate arrangement with your bank). There's no bill later —
the money is already gone the moment the transaction clears.

## What a debit card is good at

- **Zero risk of debt.** You cannot spend money you don't have, which makes it a safe default for
  anyone still building financial discipline.
- **No annual fees on most basic accounts**, and no interest to worry about, ever.
- **ATM access** — this is still a debit card's core original job.

## Where it falls short

- **No credit history built.** Using a debit card, however much and however responsibly, does
  nothing for your credit score — banks and credit bureaus only track *borrowed and repaid* money.
- **Weaker rewards.** Debit card cashback and reward programs exist but are almost always thinner
  than credit card equivalents, because the bank isn't earning interest or interchange the same way.
- **Less purchase protection.** Credit cards typically offer stronger chargeback and fraud-liability
  protections than debit cards in India, where a compromised debit card is a more direct hit to your
  actual bank balance.
