About 12% of global trade normally passes through the Red Sea and the Suez Canal — the shortest sea
route between Asia and Europe. When that route gets disrupted, the effects show up everywhere from
shipping insurance premiums to the price of a sofa, weeks later and one step removed from the
headline.

## What's actually happening, mechanically

Attacks on commercial vessels in the Red Sea have pushed a large share of shipping traffic to reroute
around the Cape of Good Hope at the southern tip of Africa — adding roughly 10–14 days and
significant fuel cost to a journey that used to go through Suez. Shipping companies made this choice
not because the alternate route is cheaper, but because insurers stopped underwriting the direct
route at any reasonable premium.

## Why this ripples out further than "shipping is slower"

Container capacity is a fixed, planned resource — ships, crews, and berthing slots are booked
months ahead. A sudden 10-day detour on a huge share of global capacity doesn't just delay goods, it
removes capacity from the system entirely for that period, which pushes freight rates up
industry-wide, not just on the affected route.

## Who actually pays for this

In the short term, shipping lines absorb some cost, but freight rate increases get passed to
importers, and from there into retail pricing with the usual delay — which is why a supply shock in
the Red Sea shows up in a shopping mall three to six months later, disconnected enough in time that
most people never trace the two together.

## What to actually watch

The insurance market's pricing for Red Sea transit is the leading indicator here — long before
shipping volumes officially recover, watch whether major insurers start underwriting the direct
route again at pre-crisis rates. That's the signal the underlying security situation has genuinely
stabilized, not just quieted down temporarily.
